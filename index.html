~ why ~

This project is dedicated to promoting positive study skills among the student body, ideally in a manner which is applicable to any other institution, yet which is nonetheless attuned to our own insofar as its particular interviews are concerned. In my academic experience up to this point, I have seen many of my peers struggle with keeping up with their work in the classroom in a manner which does not in and of itself further harm their scholastic endeavors.


Students are massively impacted by lack of access to resources which promote positive time management and study skills because their time is implicitly limited. If a student is expected to self-search and develop positive habits without any external support, they may succeed - but in doing so, they will be obliged to focus less on school work instead. By providing a resource with opinions from their peers, teachers, and other experts, though, they can find strategies within a system that promotes growth without causing even more stress to students or their schedules.


Students have a hard time finding manageable and low-stress ways to study while keeping up with their other obligations. This project aims to develop a codified resource which can offer suggestions and expedite individual exploration of potential strategies for time management and study - especially exam prep.

~ solution ~

I believe that a codified web resource for study skills and time management strategies can potentially assist students in discovering those practices and methods which most readily align with them. This may occur either through:

*the presentation of diverse, practicable strategies as complete parcels, or;

*elements thereof which a student might combine to reach a strategy well aligned with their personality and habits.


Issues

Students have a lot to keep up with, and any solution needs to easily fit in with their schedule. If it takes up more time to use than using it save, it is a waste of time. Further, it needs to fit in the environment a student works in. If the solution takes up too much of their workspace, it will detract from productivity.


Students are often eft to their own devices when finding strategies, so the solution needs to be presented in a way which makes a student more inclined to use it than simply to look on their own. It is imperative that many perspectives are demonstrated, or else some users may feel discouraged and not explore other features of the resource.


Considerations


Organization
The solution presented as many perspectives as were attainable in the development process, so that the most students possible would find strategies they aligned with included in the documentation.


Presentation
The solution was presented as a web resource so that users would not have to introduce another physical thing to their workspace - as it was felt most students would be using a computer or smartphone anyway.


Interaction
It was hoped that some modicum of interactivity would promote use of the resource in a relavent and prudent manner, to which effect timers and organizers were suggested.
~ milestones ~

The tasks required of this process were neatly subdivisible into two considerations, research and web design. In pursuit of both ends, my team and I discussed among ourselves, researched best practices, and conferred with our mentor.

Worked from the end of March through April 21st. Included acquisition of web-hosting, drafting and discussion of design parameters, and the actual writing of HyperText Markup Language, Cascading Style Sheets, and JavaScript for the web resource's operation. I led in this area, and collaborated with Xavier for design decisions.

The research process occured throughout April. Both online studies for general information and on-campus interviews for personal and local advice was undertaken. This duality was hoped to tie users in to the resource personally. This process was led by my team member Jack, and both Xavier and I collaborated to facilitate the interview process.
